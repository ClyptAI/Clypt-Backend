The Problem
Modern short-form clipping apps rely on generic models using purely stylistic virality data to create "trendy" videos. OpusClip, VEED.io, Quso.ai, Vizard.ai, and every other AI clipping tool use the same strategy:
  1. 1Recursively run and train a model on the content of millions
  2. 2Spit out timestamps matching broad structural trends
  3. 3Mash it together with 1 of 3 trending TikTok audios and neon-colored captions
  4. 4Wait for the creator to upload the next video. Repeat.
A MrBeast video gets nearly the same treatment as an Alex Hormozi business breakdown, despite their audiences being completely different. And when a topic spikes on Google, a creator's old interview subject goes viral, or a niche explodes overnight, the creator's past content teeters on the edge between complete obscurity and insane virality.
The Result
Creators
  • Lack of full-video contextual awareness
  • Clip selection that misses integral moments or frames
  • Generic music choices that don't fit their brand
  • No awareness of newly-relevant old content
Viewers
  • Clips that miss the punchline
  • Content that feels like AI-slop (same music, same captions, same energy for every creator)

THE THREE CORE FEATURES  ·  CLIPPING CORE
Content Clip · Crowd Clip · Trend Trim
01
Content Clip
Immediate, on upload
Clips based on pure content and metadata analysis combined with aggregate channel intelligence (what kind of audiences watch the creator, popular topics, content patterns). This is Clypt's baseline: it matches what current clipping tools do and layers creator-specific context on top.
Semantic Graph Architecture
  • 3 analysis streams (transcript + audio events + visual descriptions) feed into a unified semantic graph. Transcript is segmented into variable-length semantic nodes (claims, opinions, emotional beats) — not fixed time windows. Audio & visual data are similarly analyzed and bound directly onto these nodes by timestamp overlap.
  • Deterministic object tracking: YOLO11 + BoT-SORT provides persistent track IDs across the full video at native framerate. Speaker identity is fused via TalkNet, giving every node exact spatial coordinates for speaker-aware 9:16 rendering.
  • Cross-modal/video understanding: Every node knows what was said, seen, and heard simultaneously. A narrative reasoning engine classifies edges between nodes (see next page).
  • Deterministic boundaries: Clip in/out points come directly from the graph structure, not heuristic timestamp windows.
Clip Scoring
  • Sub-graph evaluation: Clips are scored as connected clusters of meaning (setup → reaction), not arbitrary time windows.
  • 6-dimension scoring: Hook (0-10), Flow, Emotional Peak, Visual Interest, Speaker Coherence, Narrative Edge.
  • Adaptive weighting: Dimension weights shift per content tone (comedy weights contradiction edges higher, tutorials weight causal edges higher, challenges weight tension/release arcs higher).
  • Hook evaluation: First 3 seconds of every clip is scored. If hook is weak, Clypt traverses the graph to find a stronger in-point.
02
Crowd Clip
Active post-publish, no upload required
Surfaces clips from audience signals: comments, retention analysis, and engagement velocity. The creator's audience has already found the best moments. Crowd Clip listens, interprets, and acts on what they're saying.
Signal Ingestion
  • Comments: Timestamped, vague, and quote references are all resolved to exact video timestamps via semantic embeddings.
  • Retention analysis: 100-point viewer activity graph showing which parts viewers stay for, seek back to, and rewatch most.
  • Engagement velocity: Time-series snapshots of views, likes, comments. Tracks acceleration, not just totals. "This video gained 50K views in the last 3 hours"
  • Audience variance: Retention at 1hr (superfans) vs. 24hr (algorithm-pushed audience), reveals which moments are favored by each demographic.
Signal Fusion
All signals fuse into convergent confidence scoring. A retention peak that also has a comment cluster + an evil laughter event + a widely grinning subject = near-certain clip. The semantic graph provides cross-modal validation: all evidence is bound per node, giving deterministic clip boundaries and full context for hook evaluation.
Autonomous operation
Crowd Clip runs without the creator doing anything. Video publishes, engagement accumulates, clips are surfaced, creator is notified. Zero manual input, zero wait time.
03
Trend Trim
Continuous, no upload required
Detects rising trends and clips autonomously when they match entities from a creator's video catalog. The world is the trigger, not the creator.
Trend Detection
  • External monitoring: Continuously scans Google Trends (Web/YouTube/News/Image Search & Shopping), X, Reddit for rising topics.
  • Per-creator relevance filtering: Auto-extracts entities from a creator's video catalog and places them on a watchlist that can be manually modified.
Full-Catalog Scan
  • Semantic search: When a trend fires, scans the entire catalog (titles, descriptions, tags, transcripts) using embeddings. Finds relevant content even without exact keyword matches.
  • Resurfacing detection: When YouTube's algorithm resurfaces an old video (sudden velocity spike), Clypt generates fresh clips from newly trending moments to ride the algorithm's wave.
Tier 1 · Macro
Super Bowl, iPhone launch, elections: massive absolute volume
Tier 2 · Niche
"Claude Opus 4.6," "Elden Ring DLC": lower volume but 2-10x relative spikes within a niche
The Moat
Current solutions are pattern-based, not event-based; they only start work when you upload a video. Trend Trim watches the world, acting as soon as an opportunity presents itself.

THE CORTEX  ·  INTELLIGENCE LAYER
Two layers of understanding: Narrative Edges · Content Mechanism Decomposition
🧠
Cortex: The Graph-Based UI
Cross-Cutting: Works with Content Clip, Crowd Clip, and Trend Trim
Every clipping tool today says "trust me, this clip will go viral." Cortex says: "Here's exactly why we think this moment matters — and if we're wrong, fix it."
  • Semantic nodes: Each node is a claim, joke, or emotional beat — bound with audioContext[], visualContext[], and decomposition context.
  • Narrative edges: Color-coded by type (see Layer 1 table). Audience overlays highlight nodes where comments cluster or retention spikes.
  • Edit & re-score: Delete bad edges, add connections, pin/exclude nodes — re-run ClipScoringAgent on the modified graph. New rankings appear instantly.
  • Prompt search: "Find where they laugh at the whiteboard" — single semantic query against multimodal-bound graph nodes.
  • Voice editing: AI editing partner via Gemini Live API. Reason through clipping decisions backed by analysis data.
Why This Matters
  • Other tools abstract away reasoning — you get a score, not an explanation.
  • When the AI guesses wrong (50%), you can't fix it — only accept or reject.
  • Cortex: Keep the 50% it gets right. Fix the 50% it doesn't. Every future clip improves.
  • Creator intent over virality prediction.
Two layers, one graph
Edges tell ClipScoringAgent what clips are possible (structure). Decompositions tell it how to score them (depth). Both are visible and editable in Cortex.
L1
Narrative Edges
Relationships BETWEEN nodes — defines what clips are possible
Edges create the graph's topology. A Callback edge between minute 2 and minute 20 creates a sub-graph ClipScoringAgent can evaluate — without it, those moments would never be clipped together.
Edge What It Connects Example
Contradiction Two claims that logically clash, or text contradicted by visual/audio evidence "I prioritize privacy" ↔ "We sell your data"
Causal Link One node establishes a problem or setup; the other resolves or extends it "Docker keeps crashing" → "Added memory limits"
Thematic Same entity or topic organically resurfaces after a digression Mentions React at min 3, tangent, React again at min 12
Tension / Release Emotional stakes escalate, then resolve with payoff or relief "24 hours to ship" → "We actually did it. It's live."
Callback Deliberate authorial reference back to an earlier moment, not organic recurrence "Remember the API I showed you?" → links to min 3
Escalation Each successive moment more extreme or intense — sustained ramp, no release yet Spice challenge: mild → hot → Carolina Reaper
Subversion Expectation is established, then broken — audience surprise, not logical clash "I followed the tutorial exactly" → total disaster
Revelation New information that fundamentally recontextualizes prior nodes "What I didn't mention is... this was all staged"
L2
Decomposition
Properties OF individual nodes — explains WHY a moment works
Enriches nodes with deeper understanding. Informs clip scoring (boundary decisions), adaptive weighting (comedy vs. tutorial vs. challenge), and what creators see in Cortex. A node can have multiple decompositions.
